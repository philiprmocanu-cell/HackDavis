# Haryana Education System — Comprehensive Context Reference

> **Purpose:** Standalone reference document for understanding the schooling landscape in Haryana, India. This file is informational only and does not affect any other project files or the application codebase.

---

## 1. Overview of the Education System Structure

Haryana follows the national school education framework established under the Right to Education (RTE) Act, 2009 and the National Education Policy (NEP) 2020. The system is structured as follows:

### 1.1 Formal School Stages

| Stage | Classes | Age Group | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-Primary / Anganwadi | Nursery–KG | 3–6 years | Managed under ICDS; not compulsory schooling |
| Primary | Class 1–5 | 6–11 years | Compulsory under RTE; near-universal enrollment |
| Upper Primary / Middle | Class 6–8 | 11–14 years | Also compulsory under RTE |
| Secondary | Class 9–10 | 14–16 years | Board exam at Class 10 (HBSE) |
| Senior Secondary | Class 11–12 | 16–18 years | Board exam at Class 12 (HBSE); streams: Science, Commerce, Arts |
| Higher Education | Degree/Diploma | 18+ years | Colleges, universities, ITIs, polytechnics |

### 1.2 Governing Bodies

- **Haryana Board of School Education (HBSE)** — Bhiwani: Conducts Class 10 and 12 board examinations for government and affiliated private schools.
- **Directorate of School Education, Haryana (DSEH):** Oversees day-to-day functioning, teacher deployment, mid-day meals, and enrollment drives.
- **Samagra Shiksha Haryana:** State implementation unit for centrally sponsored school education schemes.
- **Department of Higher Education:** Governs colleges and universities under state jurisdiction.

### 1.3 Types of Schools

- **Government Schools:** Run by the state; free education, mid-day meals, free textbooks and uniforms for students from low-income families. The majority of rural children attend these.
- **Government Aided Private Schools:** Receive government grants; follow state curriculum.
- **Private Unaided Schools:** Fee-based; more common in urban and semi-urban areas. As of ASER 2024, ~65.4% of Std II children in rural Haryana were enrolled in private schools, indicating a significant shift away from government schools at early ages.
- **Madrasa / Religious Schools:** Present in Muslim-majority districts like Nuh; some are recognized, others unrecognized and outside the state data system.
- **Kendriya Vidyalayas (KVs) and Navodaya Vidyalayas (JNVs):** Central government schools; JNVs specifically target meritorious rural students with free residential education.

## 2. How the System is Implemented

### 2.1 Enrollment and Access

- **Near-universal enrollment (Class 1–8):** The RTE Act and government schemes have driven enrollment to above 95% for ages 6–14.
- **School Mapping:** The state uses **Parivar Pehchan Patra (PPP)**, a family identity database, to map children to schools and identify out-of-school youth.
- **UDISE+ Data System:** Schools report enrollment, teacher count, and infrastructure data annually via the Unified District Information System for Education Plus portal.
- **Mid-Day Meal Scheme:** Provides a cooked meal to students enrolled in Classes 1–8 in government schools. Acts as an attendance incentive in low-income households.
- **Free Uniforms and Textbooks:** Distributed to SC/ST/BPL students in government schools through state schemes.

### 2.2 Curriculum and Teaching Approach

- **Curriculum Framework:** Haryana follows the **National Curriculum Framework (NCF)** issued by NCERT, with state-specific adaptations via SCERT Haryana.
- **Languages of Instruction:** Hindi is the primary medium of instruction. English is taught as a subject from Class 1 onward; English-medium instruction exists mainly in private schools.
- **Core Subjects (Primary):** Hindi, English, Mathematics, Environmental Studies (EVS).
- **Core Subjects (Middle/Secondary):** Hindi, English, Mathematics, Science, Social Science; electives added at Class 9–10.
- **Senior Secondary Streams:**
  - *Science:* Physics, Chemistry, Biology/Math
  - *Commerce:* Accountancy, Business Studies, Economics
  - *Arts/Humanities:* History, Geography, Political Science, Economics, Sociology, Home Science
- **Vocational Stream:** Available in some senior secondary schools (agriculture, IT, retail, healthcare) under the centrally sponsored vocational education scheme.
- **Teaching Methodology:** Predominantly teacher-centered, rote learning, and exam-focused. Classroom interaction is limited in most rural government schools. NEP 2020 calls for activity-based and competency-based learning, but implementation is slow.

### 2.3 Key Government Schemes and Initiatives

| Scheme | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Nipun Haryana** | Mission to achieve foundational literacy and numeracy (FLN) by Grade 3 by 2026-27 |
| **PM SHRI Schools** | Upgrade selected government schools with modern labs, libraries, green campuses |
| **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (BBBP)** | Launched in Haryana's Panipat district; tackles girl-child discrimination and promotes girls' education |
| **Mukhyamantri Vivah Shagun Yojana** | Financial incentive to BPL families who marry daughters after age 18 and after Class 10 |
| **Kasturba Gandhi Balika Vidyalayas (KGBV)** | Residential schools for girls from SC/ST/OBC/minority communities at Class 6–12 level |
| **Mid-Day Meal Scheme** | Daily nutrition; functions as attendance incentive |
| **RTE Section 12(1)(c)** | Reserves 25% seats in private schools for economically weaker section (EWS) children |
| **Smart Classrooms** | Digital boards and tablets deployed to government schools; partially implemented |
| **Samagra Shiksha** | Centrally funded umbrella for school education quality and access |

### 2.4 Teacher Deployment and Training

- Teachers are recruited through **HPSC** (for PGTs and Group B posts) and **HSSC** (for TGTs/PRTs).
- In-service training is conducted through **SCERT Haryana** and District Institutes of Education and Training (**DIETs**).
- Contractual teachers (known as *guest teachers* or deployed via **Haryana Kaushal Rozgar Nigam / HKRN**) are widely used to fill gaps, but lack job security and often have lower morale and attendance.
- The **Pehchaan** app and attendance management systems have been introduced to track teacher presence in schools.

## 3. Where Formal Education Ends in Practice

### 3.1 Official vs. Actual Completion

While the education system formally extends through Class 12 and beyond, the **effective end of schooling** for many Haryanvi children — especially from rural and low-income households — is significantly earlier:

| Group | Common Point of Dropout / Exit |
|---|---|
| Rural boys (poor households) | Class 8–10 (to assist in farming or migrate for labor) |
| Rural girls (general) | Class 8–10 (safety concerns, distance, family pressure) |
| Rural girls (Nuh / Mewat region) | Often Class 5–8 (child marriage, lack of nearby schools) |
| SC/OBC rural youth | Class 10 (after board exam; few continue to Class 12) |
| Urban / private school youth | Often complete Class 12; many pursue college |

### 3.2 Dropout Rates (State and District Level)

- **State dropout rate (Elementary level, Class 6–8), 2025–26:** ~3.05%
- **Nuh district dropout rate (Elementary level), 2025–26:** 12.84% (up from 8.61% in 2023–24)
- **State dropout rate (Senior Secondary, Class 11), 2025–26:** ~2.47%
- **Nuh district dropout rate (Class 11), 2025–26:** ~4.76%
- **Girls' dropout cliff:** Enrollment remains strong through Class 8 but drops sharply at Class 9 and Class 11, especially in districts without girls' hostels or nearby senior secondary schools.

### 3.3 Enrollment Funnel: Haryana Rural (Approximate)

```
Class 1  → near 100% enrollment (RTE guaranteed)
Class 5  → ~95% still enrolled
Class 8  → ~88–90% (some dropout by middle school)
Class 10 → ~75–80% (board year; many leave after this exam)
Class 12 → ~55–65% (significant attrition, especially girls in rural areas)
College  → ~30–40% of those who completed Class 12 in rural areas
```

### 3.4 Higher Education Landscape

- **Haryana has 47+ universities** (as of 2024), including state universities, deemed universities, and private universities. Key state universities include:
  - Maharshi Dayanand University (MDU), Rohtak
  - Kurukshetra University, Kurukshetra
  - Chaudhary Devi Lal University, Sirsa
  - Guru Jambheshwar University, Hisar
- **Government Colleges:** 186+ government colleges across districts. However, many rural talukas lack a nearby college, requiring students to commute or relocate — a barrier especially for girls.
- **ITIs and Polytechnics:** Industrial Training Institutes and polytechnics offer 1–2 year diploma programs in trades (electrician, fitter, plumber, tailoring, etc.). Haryana has 180+ ITIs. However, quality varies widely and rural placement links are weak.
- **Gross Enrollment Ratio (GER) in Higher Education, Haryana:** Approximately 29–32% (national GER ~28.4% per AISHE 2021–22), reflecting that roughly 2 in 3 eligible youth do not access higher education.
- **Rural-Urban Disparity:** Urban students are 2–3x more likely to enroll in college compared to rural counterparts due to proximity, coaching availability, and family expectations.

## 4. What the Education System Does NOT Teach (Knowledge Gaps)

This section documents the critical gaps between what Haryana's education system delivers and the real-world knowledge and skills its graduates — especially rural youth — actually need.

### 4.1 English Communication Skills

- English is taught as a **written grammar and translation subject**, not as a spoken language.
- Rural government school graduates typically **cannot hold a basic conversation in English** even after 12 years of formal English instruction.
- No language labs, audio resources, or conversational practice in most government schools.
- Result: Students can read passages and answer MCQs, but cannot communicate with employers, government portals, or healthcare systems that default to English.

### 4.2 Digital and Computer Literacy

- While smart classrooms have been rolled out in some schools, **internet-connected computers remain rare** in rural government primary/middle schools.
- Students may have seen a tablet but have **no systematic training** in:
  - File management, email, internet search
  - Using UPI/banking apps
  - Government portals (e.g., CSC, DigiLocker, PFMS)
  - Basic MS Office / document creation
- The **digital divide is severe**: urban private school students receive IT labs and coding from Class 6; many rural students graduate Class 12 having never used a computer independently.

### 4.3 Financial Literacy

- No standalone financial literacy subject exists in the Haryana school curriculum.
- Students exit school without understanding:
  - How to open and use a bank account
  - What Jan Dhan, PM Kisan, or MNREGA payments are
  - Loan interest calculations, compound interest (despite being taught mathematically in isolation)
  - Insurance (PM Jeevan Jyoti, PM Suraksha Bima) — what they cover, how to claim
  - Tax basics (income tax exemptions, GST on purchases)
- Financial fraud awareness is **entirely absent** from formal schooling.

### 4.4 Health and Body Literacy

- Reproductive and sexual health education is **minimal or skipped** in most rural government schools due to teacher discomfort and social conservatism.
- Students receive basic hygiene and nutrition content in EVS/Science, but lack:
  - Knowledge of menstrual hygiene management (many girls are absent during periods due to lack of facilities and knowledge)
  - Understanding of mental health: no curriculum content on stress, depression, anxiety
  - Basic first aid and emergency response
  - Knowledge of vaccination schedules, antenatal care, and newborn care
- Many girls marry young without knowing **their rights** under PCMA (child marriage law), POCSO, or domestic violence protections.

### 4.5 Agriculture and Rural Economy

- In a state where **60%+ of the rural population depends on agriculture**, the school curriculum has **no practical agriculture content** in most schools.
- Students do not learn:
  - Modern farming techniques (drip irrigation, crop rotation, soil testing)
  - How to read a mandi (wholesale market) price board
  - How PM-KISAN, crop insurance (PMFBY), or Kisan Credit Cards work
  - How to file a complaint about crop damage or land disputes
  - Animal husbandry and dairy basics
- Vocational agriculture courses exist only in select senior secondary schools and are often seen as low-status.

### 4.6 Legal and Civic Rights

- Civics is taught in Class 6–10 but focuses on constitutional theory, not **practical civic knowledge**.
- Graduates typically do not know:
  - How to register an FIR or complain against a government official
  - What the Right to Information (RTI) Act enables
  - What a gram sabha is and how to participate
  - Legal procedures for land registration, marriage registration, or death certificates
  - How to access free legal aid
  - Consumer rights (misleading advertising, defective goods)

### 4.7 Vocational and Career Guidance

- Career counseling is **absent** from most government schools.
- Students making Class 11 stream choices (Science/Commerce/Arts) do so with minimal guidance, often based on family preference or peer pressure, not aptitude.
- Awareness of career paths beyond the narrow "government job / engineering / medicine" trifecta is poor:
  - No knowledge of trades like plumbing, electrical contracting, or agri-processing as viable entrepreneurial careers
  - No awareness of government exams beyond UPSC/SSC for career planning
  - Female students rarely receive guidance on careers outside teaching and nursing

### 4.8 Critical Thinking and Problem-Solving

- The **rote learning examination system** (HBSE board pattern) rewards memorization over understanding.
- Students are trained to reproduce textbook answers, not to analyze, question, or apply knowledge.
- No formal training in:
  - Logical reasoning or argument evaluation
  - Scientific method and evidence-based thinking
  - Media literacy — students cannot distinguish reliable news from rumors or WhatsApp forwards
  - Fact-checking basic claims (e.g., health myths, scheme eligibility rumors)

### 4.9 Mental Health Awareness

- Mental health as a concept is **entirely outside the curriculum**.
- Students dealing with exam stress, family violence, gender-based discrimination, or poverty-related trauma have **no school-based support system**.
- No trained counselors in government schools (the few that exist are in urban or private institutions).
- There is significant **social stigma** around mental illness in rural Haryana that is never addressed educationally.

### 4.10 Awareness of Government Schemes

- Ironically, students graduate without knowing the **most relevant schemes for people like them**:
  - **PM Awas Yojana** (housing for BPL families)
  - **Ayushman Bharat / PMJAY** (health insurance for BPL households)
  - **Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana** (LPG for BPL women)
  - **Scholarship schemes** for SC/ST/OBC/minority students for higher education
  - **Mukhyamantri Antodaya Parivar Utthan Yojana** (Haryana's poverty uplift scheme)
- Scheme awareness is typically spread via word-of-mouth, local officials, or ration shop notices — not through schooling.

## 6. Teacher Shortage: The Ground Reality

- **Total teacher vacancies (early 2026):** 15,000+ posts across government schools
- **Breakdown:**
  - PGT (Post Graduate Teachers, Class 11–12): ~4,000 vacant
  - TGT (Trained Graduate Teachers, Class 6–10): ~7,700 vacant
  - PRT (Primary Teachers, Class 1–5): ~3,700 vacant
- **Single-teacher schools (UDISE+ 2024–25):** 1,066 schools; 43,400+ students taught by a single teacher handling all classes and subjects simultaneously
- **This number rose from 867 in 2023–24** — the problem is worsening, not improving
- **Worst affected:** Nuh district, followed by Yamunanagar, Palwal, Gurugram rural areas
- **Contractual/guest teachers:** Used widely as a stopgap; paid less, often less trained, and frequently absent due to lack of accountability

## 7. Infrastructure State of Schools

### 7.1 Physical Infrastructure

- Thousands of schools lack adequate classrooms (state assembly admitted to shortfalls in 2024–25)
- Functional toilets (especially separate girls' toilets): present in many schools on paper, but frequently locked, broken, or without water
- Some schools in Nuh district operate from open fields, community spaces (*chaupals*), or shared structures
- Boundary walls missing in many rural schools — safety concern especially for girls
- Science labs: present in ~40–50% of secondary schools; working equipment much lower
- Libraries: largely non-functional or lacking current books

### 7.2 Digital Infrastructure

- Smart classrooms and digital boards deployed to thousands of schools under state programs
- **Reality on ground:** Power cuts, broken projectors, absent internet connectivity, and untrained teachers mean most equipment sits unused
- Only ~30–40% of rural government schools have reliably functioning digital infrastructure
- Internet connectivity in schools is through government broadband (BharatNet) which is unreliable in many blocks

### 7.3 Mid-Day Meal Quality

- Mid-Day Meal is operational across Haryana but quality varies
- In some schools, meals are served cold, without variety, or with below-standard ingredients
- Caste-based discrimination in kitchen roles has been reported in some villages
- Meal acts as the primary incentive for attendance in the poorest households

---

## 8. The Private School Shift and Its Implications

- **ASER 2024:** 65.4% of Std II children in rural Haryana were in private schools — far above the national rural average
- This reflects parental perception that government schools are low-quality, not a reality of superior private school outcomes
- Private school fees range from ₹300–₹1,500/month in rural areas; affordable for aspirational lower-middle families, unaffordable for the poorest
- The children left in government schools are disproportionately from the most marginalized — SC, ST, BPL, and migrant families — with the fewest resources to supplement government education
- Private schools in rural Haryana often have:
  - English-medium instruction (even if teachers are not fluent)
  - Better hygiene and physical appearance
  - But similar teacher quality gaps and rote teaching methods

## 10. Implications for an AI Assistant Serving Haryana Users

Users interacting via SMS from Haryana may:

- Have completed only Class 8–10 of formal education
- Speak and read Haryanvi / Hindi but have low English comprehension
- Know their rights in theory but not know how to exercise them
- Be aware of schemes by name but not eligibility or application steps
- Lack financial literacy: may not understand interest rates, insurance claims, or digital banking
- Have deeply held views from family and community that may not reflect formal education
- Be first-generation school completers — their parents may be illiterate or semi-literate
- Have strong rote memory but limited exposure to analytical reasoning or fact-checking
- Need plain, concrete, locally relevant explanations — not academic or formal Hindi
- Respond better to examples using familiar items (₹, crops, local festivals, family situations) than abstract explanations

# PART A — PRIMARY STAGE (Class 1–5)

## A1. Hindi (Primary)

### A1.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Taught from Class 1–5 as the primary language of instruction in government schools.
- SCERT Haryana textbooks (or NCERT *Rimjhim* series in some schools) introduce: alphabet (varnamala), matras, simple words, two-letter / three-letter words by Class 1; short poems and prose by Class 2; basic grammar (lingadi/vachan/kaal — gender, number, tense) by Class 3; longer prose passages, letter writing, and essay basics by Class 4–5.
- Rural cultural content: folk tales, Haryanvi village settings, festivals (Teej, Holi, Diwali, Lohri), and figures from Hindi literature like Premchand are introduced.

### A1.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Decoding (reading aloud) by Class 3 in functioning schools; ASER 2024 data indicates many rural Class 3 children still cannot read a Class 2 text.
- Copy-writing and dictation (*shrut-lekh*).
- Memorization of poems and short stories.
- Basic grammar terms but limited application.

### A1.3 Teaching Methodology
- Choral reading, copy work, oral repetition.
- Strong emphasis on neat handwriting (*sundar lekh*).
- Minimal silent reading or comprehension discussion.
- Stories told by teacher; rarely student-led narration.

### A1.4 Assessment Pattern
- Spelling tests, dictation, fill-in-the-blank, copy reproduction.
- Recitation of memorized poems.
- Short-answer questions whose answers are pre-decided by the teacher.

### A1.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Reading comprehension of unfamiliar passages is weak.
- Creative writing is virtually untaught — *anuched* (paragraph) writing is template-driven.
- Listening comprehension (audio passages) is not assessed.
- Code-switching skills (between Haryanvi at home and shuddh Hindi in textbook) are not explicitly taught.
- Punctuation rules are taught mechanically; usage in real writing is poor.

### A1.6 Cap for Rural Haryana Graduate (Class 5 level)
- Can read aloud familiar text but may struggle with unfamiliar.
- Can write own name, simple sentences, and copy passages.
- Can recall memorized poems.
- Often cannot summarize a paragraph in own words.

### A1.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Use plain Hindi at Class 5 reading level; favor short sentences, common words, avoid Sanskritized vocabulary unless explained.
- Haryanvi-coded examples (village, *khet*, *chaupal*, *naukri*, *paisa*, *bhai*, *behen*) feel familiar.
- Do not assume the user can parse complex government Hindi (*sarkari* / officialese) — that register is alien even to many Class 12 graduates.

## A2. English (Primary)

### A2.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Introduced as a subject from Class 1 in Haryana government schools (since 2010s policy).
- Class 1–2: alphabet, phonics (limited), simple words (cat, dog, pen), color names, numbers.
- Class 3–5: short poems, prose passages, basic grammar (noun, verb, tense), simple sentence patterns.
- Textbook: NCERT *Marigold* series or SCERT-adapted equivalents.

### A2.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Recognition of alphabet and ability to copy letters.
- A small set of memorized words and rhymes.
- ASER 2024: most rural Class 5 children in Haryana cannot read a Class 2 English text fluently.

### A2.3 Teaching Methodology
- Translation method: teacher reads English aloud, translates each line into Hindi.
- Heavy use of choral repetition.
- Almost no spoken English practice; teacher's own English fluency is often limited.
- No phonics-based reading instruction in most government primary schools.

### A2.4 Assessment Pattern
- Word-meaning matching, fill-in-the-blanks, copy-writing.
- Recitation of poems.
- Spelling tests.

### A2.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Listening and speaking are entirely absent from assessment, so teachers de-prioritize them.
- No audio resources or language labs in most rural schools.
- Phonics is replaced by rote letter-name memorization.
- Confidence to attempt spoken English is destroyed early by ridicule from peers/teachers.

### A2.6 Cap for Rural Haryana Graduate (Class 5 level)
- Can recognize and copy English alphabet and ~50–200 words.
- Cannot construct an original sentence in English.
- Cannot understand spoken English at any speed beyond memorized phrases.

### A2.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Default to Hindi/Haryanvi for any user who writes in romanized Hindi or whose English is purely keyword-level.
- When English is unavoidable (form fields, scheme names, drug names, error messages), include the Hindi gloss in parentheses.
- Do not interpret a single English keyword as evidence of English fluency — many users type "BANK" or "CARD" because the original word entered their vocabulary that way.

## A3. Mathematics (Primary)

### A3.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Class 1–2: numbers 1–100, addition and subtraction (single digit), shapes, comparison (bigger/smaller), basic time (morning/evening).
- Class 3: multiplication tables (2–10), simple division, money (rupees, paise), measurement (cm, m, kg, gm, litre).
- Class 4: fractions (introduction), four-digit numbers, addition/subtraction with carry/borrow, simple word problems.
- Class 5: decimals (introduction), factors and multiples, simple geometry (triangle, square, circle), perimeter and area of rectangle, time and date arithmetic.

### A3.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Number recognition and basic operations.
- Times tables (rote).
- ASER 2024: many rural Class 5 children in Haryana cannot do simple division (e.g., 56 ÷ 4).

### A3.3 Teaching Methodology
- Drill-and-practice on procedural arithmetic.
- Word problems read aloud and solved on the board; students copy.
- Manipulatives (counters, base-10 blocks) rarely used despite being prescribed.

### A3.4 Assessment Pattern
- Computation problems with whole-number answers.
- Word problems with predictable templates ("Ram has 5 mangoes…").

### A3.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Conceptual understanding of place value is weak — students do operations procedurally without understanding why.
- Estimation skills are not taught (rural users often cannot say "is 47 × 6 closer to 300 or 500?").
- Number sense around money beyond ₹100 transactions is shaky.
- Word problems that deviate from textbook patterns are missed.

### A3.6 Cap for Rural Haryana Graduate (Class 5 level)
- Can add and subtract three-digit numbers with effort.
- Can multiply by single digits using memorized tables.
- Division and fraction comparison are unreliable.

### A3.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Always show calculations stepwise: "₹150 × 4 = ₹600. Plus ₹50 fee = ₹650 total."
- For interest, EMI, or scheme math, use small concrete numbers and visual layouts (rupee figures rather than abstract %).
- Never assume the user can mentally compute a percentage, even one as simple as "10% of ₹2,000."

## A4. Environmental Studies (EVS, Class 3–5)

### A4.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Integrated subject combining science, social science, and life skills.
- Themes: family, neighborhood, food, water, shelter, animals, plants, our body, health, work and play, festivals, transport, communication.
- NCERT *Looking Around* series or SCERT Haryana textbook.

### A4.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Names of body parts, common animals, common crops.
- Names of family relationships (chacha, mausi, taya, mami).
- Festivals and their broad descriptions.
- Simple food groups and the importance of clean water.

### A4.3 Teaching Methodology
- Picture-based teaching; children identify items in textbook illustrations.
- Field visits and project work prescribed but rarely done.
- Lessons usually delivered as oral lectures plus copy work.

### A4.4 Assessment Pattern
- Match-the-following, fill-in-the-blanks, single-line answers.
- Drawings (e.g., draw a plant and label parts).

### A4.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Hygiene practices are described but rarely connected to school-level practice (toilets, hand-washing stations may be locked or broken).
- Water and waste are taught topically but not connected to local realities (waterlogging, open defecation, plastic burning).
- Health content is generic and skips menstruation, puberty, mental health.
- Environmental/climate content is thin; no sense of why kharif/rabi seasons are shifting in Haryana.

### A4.6 Cap for Rural Haryana Graduate (Class 5 level)
- Can name body parts, common plants and animals, basic family relations, common festivals.
- Knows that water should be clean and food should be cooked, but struggles to identify warning signs (e.g., turbidity, expiry).
- Limited understanding of disease transmission.

### A4.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Health and hygiene advice should reference concrete actions ("boil water for 5 minutes if it looks cloudy after rain") rather than principles.
- For schemes related to sanitation (Swachh Bharat) or water (Jal Jeevan Mission), explain what the scheme physically delivers (taps, toilets) rather than the policy framework.

### A5.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Officially part of the timetable but treated as filler periods in most government primary schools.

### A5.5 Implications for AI Agent

# PART B — UPPER PRIMARY / MIDDLE STAGE (Class 6–8)

## B1. Hindi (Middle)

### B1.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Two textbooks per year: *Vasant* (literature) and *Doorva* / *Bharti* (language and grammar) per NCERT pattern; SCERT Haryana adapts similar.
- Literature: prose and poetry by Premchand, Tulsidas, Kabir, Mahadevi Verma, Harivansh Rai Bachchan, plus contemporary writers.
- Grammar: parts of speech (sangya, sarvanaam, kriya, visheshan), tenses (kaal), gender (ling), number (vachan), case (kaarak), simple/compound/complex sentences.
- Composition: essay (*nibandh*), letter (*patra*), application (*prarthna patra*), report (*report-lekhan*), notice (*soochna*).

### B1.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Decent reading comprehension at the level of textbook chapters (because they have been pre-explained).
- Templates for the formal letter, application, and notice (which appear in the board exam).
- Memorized definitions of grammatical terms.

### B1.3 Teaching Methodology
- Teacher reads chapter, explains line-by-line, students copy *prashna-uttar* (Q&A) into notebooks.
- Grammar is taught as definitions plus identification exercises.
- Literature interpretation is dictated by teacher; alternate readings discouraged.

### B1.4 Assessment Pattern
- Short and long answer questions on textbook chapters with pre-decided answers.
- Grammar identification and transformation (active to passive, etc.).
- Composition based on memorized templates.

### B1.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Critical reading of unseen passages is weak.
- Free expression in Hindi (writing one's own opinion, not a stock answer) is not practiced.
- Hindi-language news, government documents, and legal text use a register (Sanskritized, *pratiyogi-Hindi*) the student may not actively read.
- Literary analysis is recipe-based; figurative language identification is rote.

### B1.6 Cap for Class 8 Graduate
- Reads simple Hindi prose and writes paragraphs on familiar topics.
- Can fill out forms in Hindi if the form is plain (not officialese).
- Struggles with court orders, RTI replies, and legal notices in Hindi.

### B1.7 Implications for AI Agent
- For complex government / legal / banking content, translate from officialese to plain Hindi: "*Aapke liye ye lagu nahi hai*" instead of "*Yeh apke prasang mein anuprayojya nahi hai*."
- Define heavy-Sanskritized terms when first used (*adhinyaay*, *pratibhuti*, *vimochan*) in parentheses.

## B2. English (Middle)

### B2.1 Classes & Curriculum
- NCERT *Honeysuckle* / *Honeycomb* / *It So Happened* series (or SCERT equivalents).
- Reading: short stories, poems, and articles.
- Grammar: tense, voice, narration, articles, prepositions, modals, simple/compound sentences.
- Composition: paragraph writing, informal and formal letters, story writing, notice, message, dialogue completion.

### B2.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Recognition of English words and ability to translate textbook chapters with help.
- Some grammatical terms but inconsistent application.
- Templates for formal letter / notice as required by board pattern.

### B2.3 Teaching Methodology
- Translation-based teaching: every English line rendered into Hindi.
- Listening/speaking absent; pronunciation rarely corrected.
- Composition is template-fed: the formal letter has a fixed structure students memorize.

### B2.4 Assessment Pattern
- Reading comprehension on familiar passages.
- Grammar transformation.
- Composition that mirrors templates.

### B2.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Spoken English ability remains close to zero in rural government schools.
- Listening to varied accents (US / UK / Indian English) is not practiced.
- Nuance, idiom, and pragmatic English (asking, refusing politely, negotiating) are absent.
- Unfamiliar vocabulary breaks reading comprehension entirely.

### B2.6 Cap for Class 8 Graduate
- Can read simple English texts with effort; usage of dictionary or translation is heavy.
- Cannot write a free-form English email or letter without a template.
- Cannot understand colloquial spoken English.

### B2.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Treat English-medium SMS as a translation aid for users — even if they accept English, mirror in Hindi for safety.
- Government-portal English ("OTP," "credit," "verified," "transaction declined") is alien — explain meaning, not just literal translation.

## B3. Sanskrit (Third Language)

### B3.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Compulsory third language in many Haryana schools from Class 6–8 (sometimes optional with Punjabi).
- Curriculum: Devnagari script (already known), basic vocabulary, *shabd-roop* (noun declensions), *dhatu-roop* (verb conjugations), short *shlokas*, prose passages.
- Cultural exposure: stories from Mahabharata, Ramayana, Panchatantra in Sanskrit form.

### B3.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Reading aloud Sanskrit text (transliteration is no barrier since script is shared with Hindi).
- Memorization of basic *shabd-roop* (e.g., balak, balako, balakaha) and *dhatu-roop* (paath, pathati, pathanti).
- Translation of simple sentences with teacher's help.

### B3.3 Teaching Methodology
- Heavy memorization of declension and conjugation tables.
- Teacher dictates translations; students copy.
- Almost no contextual reading or composition.

### B3.4 Assessment Pattern
- Fill-in-the-blank with declined forms.
- Translation of simple sentences (Sanskrit-to-Hindi).
- Recitation of *shlokas*.

### B3.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Sanskrit's role in modern Indian languages is not taught; students don't connect *putra* (Sanskrit) to *putr* (Hindi).
- Cultural texts are presented as fixed truths; analytical or historical reading is absent.
- Compositional Sanskrit is not expected even at Class 10 level.

### B3.6 Cap for Class 8 Graduate
- Can read Sanskrit *shlokas* aloud.
- Memorized a small set of grammatical forms.
- No real ability to read or write original Sanskrit.

### B3.7 Implications for AI Agent
- A user with strong Sanskrit exposure may parse heavily Sanskritized Hindi better than expected — but do not over-rely on this; default to plain.
- Religious/cultural references in Sanskrit-derived Hindi are recognized but not always understood semantically.

### B4.3 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Composition and analytical Punjabi are weak.

### B4.4 Cap for Class 8 Graduate
- Can read and write Gurmukhi at functional level.

### B4.5 Implications for AI Agent
- For users in Sirsa/Yamunanagar/Ambala border zones, Punjabi-coded examples land well.

## B5. Mathematics (Middle)

### B5.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Class 6: integers, fractions, decimals, ratio and proportion, basic algebra (linear expressions), elementary geometry, perimeter/area, simple data handling.
- Class 7: rational numbers, exponents and powers, algebraic expressions, simple equations, congruence of triangles, perimeter/area of triangle/parallelogram/circle, data handling.
- Class 8: rational numbers (advanced), linear equations, quadrilaterals, mensuration (cube/cuboid/cylinder), exponents, factorization, direct and inverse proportion, comparing quantities (percentage, profit/loss, simple interest, compound interest at introductory level), graphs.

### B5.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Basic operations on integers, fractions, decimals.
- Solving linear equations in one variable.
- Profit/loss/percentage/simple-interest calculation by formula.
- Area and perimeter of common shapes.

### B5.3 Teaching Methodology
- Procedural drill: copy formula → substitute → compute.
- Word problems are template-recognition exercises.
- Conceptual reasoning ("why does this formula work?") is rare.

### B5.4 Assessment Pattern
- Computation, simple word problems, formula-based questions.
- Two-step problems (combining operations) are the upper bound for most.

### B5.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Application of percentage to real-life situations (GST, discount stacking, taxation) is shaky.
- Simple interest is often confused with compound interest.
- Algebraic reasoning (manipulating an equation to isolate a variable in a non-textbook setting) is poor.
- Number sense for large numbers (lakh, crore conversion) is mechanical, not intuitive.

### B5.6 Cap for Class 8 Graduate
- Can compute simple interest given P, R, T.
- Can compute discount and final price for a single percentage step.
- Cannot reliably compute compound interest, EMI, or stacked discounts without help.

### B5.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Show all interest math step by step, including unit conversion.
- Use ₹-denominated examples that the user could plausibly encounter (₹5,000 loan, ₹200 discount, not ₹10,00,000 mortgage).

## B6. Science (Middle, Combined)

### B6.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Single integrated subject covering Physics, Chemistry, and Biology themes.
- Class 6: food and components, fibres and fabrics, materials, separation of substances, changes around us, getting to know plants, body movements, living organisms and habitats, motion and measurement, light and shadows, electric circuit, water, air, garbage in/out.
- Class 7: nutrition in plants and animals, fibre to fabric, heat, acids/bases/salts, physical and chemical changes, weather and climate, winds-storms-cyclones, soil, respiration, transportation, reproduction in plants, motion and time, electric current, light, water management, forests, wastewater.
- Class 8: crop production, microorganisms, synthetic fibres, materials (metals/non-metals), coal and petroleum, combustion and flame, conservation of plants and animals, cell, reproduction in animals, reaching the age of adolescence, force and pressure, friction, sound, chemical effects of electric current, some natural phenomena (lightning, earthquakes), light, stars and the solar system, pollution of air and water.

### B6.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Definitions of key terms (e.g., "force is a push or a pull").
- Diagrams and labels (cell, plant parts, digestive system).
- Lists of facts (states of matter, types of fibres).

### B6.3 Teaching Methodology
- Theoretical instruction; lab work is largely absent in rural government schools.
- Diagrams are copied from textbook to notebook.
- Demonstration experiments are rare due to lack of equipment.

### B6.4 Assessment Pattern
- Definitions, diagrams (label), short and long answer, fill-in-the-blanks.
- Almost no application questions.

### B6.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Scientific method, hypothesis-testing, and skepticism are not built.
- Hands-on lab skills are absent — many Class 8 graduates have never used a Bunsen burner, beaker, or microscope.
- Biology related to human reproduction (Class 8 *adolescence* chapter) is often skipped or rushed due to teacher discomfort.
- Connection between science and daily life (e.g., why immunization works, why fertilizers harm soil) is weak.

### B6.6 Cap for Class 8 Graduate
- Can name parts of plant, body systems, types of materials.
- Can recall textbook examples but struggles with application.
- Often holds folk beliefs (about disease, food, eclipses) alongside textbook content without conflict.

### B6.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Health, hygiene, and disease information should be presented with concrete actions, not mechanism-first.
- Be ready to address common myths (e.g., "eclipse causes pregnancy harm," "menstruating women shouldn't enter kitchens," "polio drops cause infertility") with respect, not condescension.

- Lists of dates, dynasties, kings, and battles.
- Names of major rivers, mountains, cities of India.

- Names of revolutions, leaders, treaties, dates.
- Indian river systems, soil types, crop seasons, manufacturing centers.
- Names of constitutional bodies and electoral basics.
- Definitions of GDP, inflation, sectors of economy.

### C5.3 Teaching Methodology
- Lecture and dictation; some map work and project work in better-resourced schools.
- Sample-paper drilling for board exam.

### C5.4 Assessment Pattern
- HBSE board paper with MCQ, short and long answer, source-based questions, map work.

### C5.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Civic procedures (FIR, RTI, gram sabha participation, accessing records) are taught conceptually only.
- Consumer rights are named but not operationalized (how to file a complaint).
- Money and credit chapter introduces banks and credit but does not teach UPI, KCC, or fraud awareness.
- Economic concepts like inflation, interest rates, exchange rates are defined but not connected to household decisions.

### C5.6 Cap for Class 10 Graduate
- Knows the structure of Indian government and key constitutional concepts.
- Knows India has a banking system and major economic sectors.
- Cannot translate any of this into an actionable step (filing a complaint, comparing loan offers, understanding a Jan Dhan account).

### C5.7 Implications for AI Agent
- Translate civic and economic concepts into concrete steps with named offices, forms, and timelines.
- Don't assume the user can self-navigate from "I have a right to RTI" to actually filing one.

# PART D — SENIOR SECONDARY, SCIENCE STREAM (Class 11–12)

## D1. Physics

### D1.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Class 11: physical world, units and measurement, motion in a straight line, motion in a plane, laws of motion, work-energy-power, system of particles, rotational motion, gravitation, mechanical properties of solids and fluids, thermal properties, thermodynamics, kinetic theory, oscillations, waves.
- Class 12: electric charges and fields, electrostatic potential, current electricity, magnetism, electromagnetic induction, AC, electromagnetic waves, ray and wave optics, dual nature of radiation and matter, atoms, nuclei, semiconductors, communication systems (older syllabus).

### D1.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Formulas, derivations, numericals.
- Standard JEE-pattern problem types if the school or coaching covers them.

### D1.3 Teaching Methodology
- Lecture + numerical drill.
- Lab work for board practical exam (often coached, not conducted authentically).

### D1.4 Assessment Pattern
- HBSE board: theory + practical.
- Heavy weightage to numerical problems and derivations.

### D1.5 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Practical instrumentation skills are weak.
- Conceptual understanding (why does this work?) often takes a back seat to formula recall.
- Cross-cutting connections (e.g., from electromagnetism to motors) are skipped unless the teacher is exceptional.

### D1.6 Cap for Class 12 Graduate (Science, Haryana average)
- Can solve standard numericals, recall key derivations.
- Cannot translate Physics knowledge into engineering judgment without further training.

### D1.7 Implications for AI Agent
- A Class 12 Science graduate can engage with technical reasoning if walked through stepwise.
- Do not assume practical electronics literacy (wiring, basic safety, voltage rating) — that's a workshop skill, not a Physics-class skill.

## D2. Chemistry

### D2.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Class 11: basic concepts, structure of atom, classification of elements, chemical bonding, states of matter, thermodynamics, equilibrium, redox reactions, hydrogen, s-block, p-block (groups 13–14), organic chemistry basics, hydrocarbons, environmental chemistry.
- Class 12: solid state, solutions, electrochemistry, chemical kinetics, surface chemistry, isolation of elements, p-block (15–18), d- and f-block, coordination compounds, haloalkanes/haloarenes, alcohols/phenols/ethers, aldehydes/ketones/carboxylic acids, amines, biomolecules, polymers, chemistry in everyday life.

### D2.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Periodic table trends, balancing equations, reaction mechanisms (limited).
- Standard numericals on mole concept, equilibrium, electrochemistry.

### D2.3 Teaching Methodology
- Lecture + reaction-mechanism memorization.
- Lab work focused on board practical exam (titration, salt analysis).

### D2.4 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Real-world chemistry (water testing, soil pH, fertilizer composition) is rarely connected.
- Industrial and environmental chemistry are theoretical.
- Lab safety culture is weak.

### D2.5 Cap for Class 12 Graduate (Science)
- Can recall reactions and run a basic titration.
- May not connect chemistry to drug labels, fertilizer NPK ratios, or water quality.

### D2.6 Implications for AI Agent
- For pesticide, fertilizer, and pharmaceutical guidance, give plain-language interpretation of labels.
- Don't assume Class 12 chemistry maps to safe handling of farm chemicals or medicines.

## D3. Biology

### D3.1 Classes & Curriculum
- Class 11: diversity of living organisms, structural organization in plants and animals, cell structure and function, plant physiology, human physiology.
- Class 12: reproduction (in organisms, sexual reproduction in flowering plants, human reproduction, reproductive health), genetics and evolution, biology and human welfare (health and disease, microbes, food production), biotechnology, ecology and environment.

### D3.2 What Students Actually Learn
- Diagrams, processes (photosynthesis, respiration, digestion), classification.
- Mendelian genetics (basic), reproductive cycles, ecology basics.

### D3.3 Teaching Methodology
- Diagram-heavy and definition-heavy delivery.
- Reproductive health chapter is in syllabus but often rushed/skipped.

### D3.4 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Practical biology (microscopy, dissection alternatives) is uneven.
- Reproductive and sexual health is sanitized; contraception, STIs, consent are barely engaged.
- Public-health and epidemiology framing is absent.

### D3.5 Cap for Class 12 Graduate (Science)
- Can name and describe biological processes and diagrams.
- May still hold cultural taboos and folk beliefs about menstruation, fertility, and disease alongside textbook knowledge.

### D3.6 Implications for AI Agent
- Reproductive health questions should be answered respectfully and concretely; assume the user has no prior frank guidance.
- Public-health (vaccine, immunization, antenatal care) explanations should anchor on schedule and access ("get it at the PHC", "free under Mission Indradhanush").

### D4.6 Implications for AI Agent

## H4. Career Counseling (or absence thereof)

### H4.1 Status
- Almost entirely absent from Haryana government schools.
- Class 11 stream choice (Science/Commerce/Arts) is made by family preference and grades, not aptitude.

### H4.2 Knowledge Gaps / Limitations
- Awareness of career paths beyond UPSC / SSC / engineering / medicine / teaching is low.
- Female students are funneled toward teaching, nursing, or domestic roles.
- Knowledge of skill-based careers (electrician contracting, dairy entrepreneur, agri-processor, food vlogger) is poor.

### H4.3 Implications for AI Agent
- Career advice should explicitly broaden the option set: government exams beyond UPSC/SSC (Banking PO, Indian Railways NTPC, Haryana SSC, Haryana Police, AIIMS Nursing, Para-medical, ITIs, Skill India, NIELIT certifications).
- For self-employment/entrepreneurship paths, provide specific walkthroughs (Mudra loan, Udyam registration, e-Shram for unorganized workers, ONDC for sellers).

# PART I — CROSS-CUTTING LIMITATIONS

## I.1 Pedagogy
- Rote memorization is the dominant cognitive demand across nearly all subjects.
- Project-based or inquiry-based learning is rare in rural government schools.
- Critical thinking / argument analysis is largely absent across the curriculum.

## I.2 Practical and Lab Skills
- Science labs in rural government schools are under-equipped, often locked, or used only for the practical exam.
- Computer labs face the same fate.
- Vocational-stream practical exposure depends entirely on the individual school.

## I.3 Languages
- Hindi is the medium; reading/writing fluency is functional for most Class 10 graduates.
- English is studied for 12 years but conversational fluency is rare in rural cohorts.
- Sanskrit/Punjabi exposure is decoding-level, rarely productive.

## I.4 Numeracy and Data Literacy
- Procedural arithmetic is acceptable up to Class 10.
- Estimation, statistical reasoning, probabilistic thinking, and data interpretation are weak.

## I.5 Digital Literacy
- Even with IT subject, real digital fluency (file management, email, secure password, scam recognition, payments, government portals) is uneven and often absent.

## I.6 Civic and Procedural Knowledge
- Concepts of government, rights, and schemes are taught.
- Procedural how-to (forms, offices, timelines, escalation) is largely absent.

## I.7 Health, Body, and Mind
- Hygiene basics covered.
- Reproductive, sexual, and mental-health content is skipped, sanitized, or rushed.

## I.8 Career and Livelihood
- Career counseling is absent.
- Livelihood-relevant skills (entrepreneurship operations, e-commerce, vocational trades-as-business) are at concept level only.

## I.9 Critical / Media Literacy
- Distinguishing reliable news from rumor / WhatsApp forwards is not taught.
- Fact-checking skills are absent.
- Susceptibility to scams, misinformation, and political mobilization via misinformation is high.

## I.10 Local Knowledge
- The curriculum is national/standardized; local Haryana conditions (groundwater, stubble, gender ratio, caste politics, mandi system) are rarely engaged in the school context.

# PART J — SYNTHESIS: WHAT EVERY RURAL HARYANA GRADUATE LIKELY LACKS

A typical rural Haryana Class 10 / Class 12 graduate from a government school is likely to be:

**Confident in:**
- Basic Hindi reading and writing.
- Basic arithmetic and money math at simple-interest level.
- Names of major schemes, leaders, and constitutional bodies.
- Recognition of major crops, festivals, family relationships.
- One or two memorized templates for formal letter, application, and notice.

**Shaky in:**
- Free-form writing, original opinion-expression in any language.
- Compound interest, EMI, percentage stacking.
- Connecting science textbook concepts to local agriculture, environment, or health.
- Reading and interpreting officialese (legal notices, bank documents, RTI replies).
- English speaking, listening, and free-form writing.

**Likely missing entirely:**
- Personal financial planning (budget, savings, insurance choice).
- Operational digital skills (email, secure payments, scam recognition, app navigation beyond WhatsApp/UPI/YouTube).
- Practical health literacy (menstrual hygiene, contraception, mental health, vaccination schedule beyond basics, drug labels).
- Civic procedure operationalization (filing FIR, RTI, complaint, gram sabha participation).
- Career-path mapping beyond the narrow government-job / engineering / medicine / teaching trifecta.
- Entrepreneurship operationals (Udyam, GST, Mudra, e-Shram, ONDC, marketing, customer acquisition).
- Critical media literacy (rumor vs news, fact-checking, identifying phishing).
- Mental health vocabulary, self-care strategies, and help-seeking pathways.

**Important nuance:**
- Class 12 Science graduates from rural Haryana are typically slightly more numerate and digitally literate than peers, but the practical-skill gap remains.
- Class 12 Commerce graduates know more business vocabulary but rarely operate the actual MSME / GST / digital business stack.
- Class 12 Arts graduates know civic and social vocabulary but rarely operate procedures.
- Vocational-track graduates have entry-level trade skills but limited business-operations literacy.

# PART K — OPERATIONAL HOOKS FOR THE SMS-AI AGENT

When the downstream agent receives a query, it can use the following heuristics — calibrated against the caps documented above — to decide depth and tone:

## K.1 Quick Diagnostic Signals

- **User writes in Roman Hindi (Hinglish):** Most likely Class 8–12 rural graduate, comfortable in Hindi, partial English keyword vocabulary. Default Hindi response, mirror keywords in English.
- **User writes in Devanagari Hindi:** Strong Hindi literacy. Plain Hindi response works.
- **User writes mixed Hindi-English with Indian spelling ("bcoz," "tym," "wat"):** Younger, more digitally exposed, but English fluency is shallow. Use simple English / Hindi mix.
- **User uses pure formal English with correct grammar:** Likely urban Class 12+ graduate or college-going. Can engage in English.
- **User asks for a scheme by name:** Has heard of it but probably does not know eligibility, documents, or steps. Walk through procedure concretely.
- **User asks about a number (interest, EMI, GST, percentage):** Show stepwise math; do not skip steps.

## K.2 Default Adjustments by Topic

- **Money / banking / loans:** Always show step-by-step math. Always name documents required (Aadhaar, PAN, bank passbook, photo, residence proof). Always mention timelines and escalation if the office stalls.
- **Health / medicine:** Use plain Hindi labels; explain abbreviations (BP, BS, Hb). For mental health, normalize first; provide helpline numbers (KIRAN 1800-599-0019).
- **Government schemes:** State eligibility, documents, where to apply (CSC, online portal, block office), and what to do if rejected.
- **Agriculture:** Reference local season (kharif/rabi), local crops, local mandi, current MSP if known. Respect existing family practice.
- **Education / career:** Broaden the option set explicitly. Don't reinforce the UPSC/engineering/teaching-only trifecta unless the user asks specifically.
- **Legal / civic:** Translate from Constitution-vocabulary to "go to this office, fill this form, escalate to this official."
- **Digital onboarding:** Step-by-step. Assume zero prior experience with the app.

## K.3 Tone

- Address the user with respect (default *aap*, not *tum*).
- Avoid English jargon unless paired with Hindi gloss.
- Avoid academic register; favor concrete examples in ₹, kg, *mausam*, *gaon* terms.
- Acknowledge family/community values when giving advice that might conflict with them; do not lecture.

## K.4 Red Flags Where the Agent Must Slow Down

- **Financial-fraud-related queries:** User may already be at risk; assume scam-vulnerable and walk through verification slowly.
- **Health emergencies:** Triage first; route to ASHA worker, PHC, or 108 ambulance.
- **Mental-health distress:** Normalize, provide helpline, suggest one trusted person to talk to.
- **Land / dowry / domestic-violence indicators:** Provide rights information AND escalation path (1098, 181, legal aid services) without judgment.
